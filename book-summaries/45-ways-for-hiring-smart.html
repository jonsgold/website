Book Summary
Introduction
  1. A mistake in hiring, rectified within 6 months by replacing that employee, costs 2.5 the person's annual salary.
  2. Example of Tony Razzano, chief scout for the San Francisco 49ers in the 1980s - he drafted Joe Montana and Jerry Rice.
  3. His system was to see 200 plays of the eligible player - watch him in real live situations.
  4. The best predictor of future behavior is past behavior.
  5. The pre-interview strategies are designed to eliminate up to 80% of inappropriate applicants.
Chapter 1: Pre-Interview Strategies
  1. Most interviewees prepare for an interview.
  2. So should the company script some pre-interview plays for which there can be little preparation by the candidate.
  3. A less predictable interview allows to more accurately evaluate performance on the job.
  4. Also crucial to evaluate if it will be a good long-term relationship.
Strategy 1: Make Phone Contact with the Candidate
  1. How hard or easy is it to reach the candidate?
  2. Paradox - some good candidates are uncomfortable on the phone / most con men are terrific on the phone.
  3. Asleep after lunch? Probably a red light.
  4. Review time, place and dress for initial interview.
  5. Example of NY executive in SF interview - didn't follow directions, was late by 1.5 hours without calling, and dressed in a suit.
  6. 5 to 10% of interviewees will screw up this first test.
Strategy 2: Ask for a Letter and Resume
  1. Is the candidate prompt / literate / neat?
  2. People lie on their resumes - about academic credentials, job responsibilities, previous compensation.
  3. Example of candidate who never graduated from Vanderbilt University.
  4. Example of candidate with 4 year gap - was in jail for murder.
  5. Example of candidate who omitted a past job. Instead of lying, he should have admitted to having had a bad relationship with that boss.
  6. Deceptive behavior on a resume is a red light.
Strategy 3: Give an Assignment Before the Interview
  1. Ask the candidate to visit a store / office / web page before the interview then ask for his / her observations.
  2. Demonstrates how the person carries out a task. A candidate that offers insight into the business has spoken volumes before the interview.
  3. Obviously will give a rave review - but what else does he / she say? Is it helpful, specific, accurate?
  4. Example of candidate's report to the American Golf Corporation, with specific suggestions which were immediately implemented (he got the job).
  5. Example of candidate's report to an upstate NY bank, with insightful suggestions for improvement in customer service (she got the job).
  6. They did not give the impression they would come in and change everything before getting to know operations better.
Strategy 4: Walk Around the Office When the Candidate Arrives
  1. To make small talk and lower anxieties.
  2. Look for curiosity - does the candidate ask questions?
  3. Curiosity is a valuable asset, it demonstrates a desire to discover new things - a good sign for a candidate's success.
  4. Nervousness can lead to no questions asked, but absolutely no questions can give a sense of a person's curiosity level.
  5. Example of Crate and Barrel company - walked around with COO candidate to see how he reacts with people, and their impression of him.
Strategy 5: Read Resumes in Teams if Possible
  1. One team member will emerge as the resume expert.
  2. Teams that work together are more accurate and insightful than individuals - depth of reading.
  3. Example of candidate that worked in Japan and France, but had 9 months of unemployment.
  4. Discussion was quick and more complete (different views, one team member knew Japan) than with single reader.
  5. Teams can also waste time / pass over good candidates if they "must have consensus" or a dominant member monopolises the discussion.
Strategy 6: Cast the Widest Net Possible
  1. Improve the candidate pool to raise the bar and get better finalists.
  2. Let wide range of people know you're looking for better candidates.
  3. Keep excellent candidates on record - ask them if they know anyone, maybe they'll suggest themselves.
  4. Recognise excellent people wherever you find them:
    1. Example of financial whiz found at dinner party that interacted brilliantly with everyone.
    2. Example of hotel assistant manager that dealt with difficult customer.
  5. Microsoft's recruiting "Strike Team" is on the phones from 6:00 - 20:00.
  6. The best candidates are not looking for new jobs.
Strategy 7: Use Caution Around Any Big Changes
  1. Hiring from big companies to little companies (and other drastic differences) can be dangerous.
  2. An olympic sprinter doesn't compete in the mile run.
  3. Begin search for talent within industry / culture / business.
  4. Also look for less experienced talent within your organisation.
  5. There are exceptions, but start here.
Strategy 8: Rethink the Position Before the Interview
  1. Before interviewing for an old job, it's an excellent time to rethink the job itself.
  2. Example of last creative director that couldn't administer his department - why not split the job: one creative, one administrative.
  3. Example of Pentagram 3-day retreat: office administrator brought in to work on logistics.
  4. While rethinking the position, might discover the job isn't necessary.
  5. Rethinking the position is synonymous with streamlining the position, can the person be utilised more effectively?
Strategy 9: Use Pre-Interview Tips in Combinations
  1. Integrating several strategies will eventually save you a lot of time.
  2. Example of non-profit seeking office manager:
    1. Cast widest net possible. Placed ad asking for resume and cover letter.
    2. Ask for cover letter. 20% of candidates were eliminated because they didn't write one.
    3. Read resumes in teams. Funneled resumes into 3 stacks - pile A would get phone call, pile B maybe, pile C rejects (e.g. with typos or not qualified).
    4. Make phone contact with applicants. From pile A, found out if easy or hard to reach the candidate, what were his phone skills?
Strategy 10: Conduct a Brief Pre-Interview Interview
  1. Helpful in identifying paper tigers - people with great resumes but less impressive in person.
  2. Example of inappropriate candidate in red Mustang convertible and miniskirt for non-profit that works with adolescents - saved hours of interview time.
  3. Example of candidate with good letter and phone interview that was just ok - but the pre-interview was great with good chemistry. Eventually hired.
  4. Summary of strategies 9 and 10 used by non-profit:
    1. Cover letter and resume screening into three piles, team reading.
    2. Telephone screening, done by intuitive person.
    3. Short pre-interview, done by directors.
    4. Reference checking of finalists, done by three people.
    5. Final interviews, project assignment.
    6. Final decision.
Chapter 2: Strategies During the Interview
  1. Results of an interview may be misleading, only showing the candidate passed the chemistry and interview tests.
  2. A good con artist can fool you every time.
  3. Interviews with induced stress don't work - putting a candidate on the defensive will only put up walls and demonstrate his defensive behavior.
Strategy 11: Trust Your Instincts - Chemistry Is Crucial
  1. Assuming you've been successful predicting winners in the past, trust your gut.
  2. Usually determined in the first few minutes of an interview: it's either good, bad or absent.
  3. Where chemistry is important and it's bad or absent, cut the interview short.
  4. Prepare all candidates in advance that the initial meeting will be 15 - 20 minutes just to meet and say hello.
  5. Example of Harry V. Quadracci: never do business with anybody you don't like.
  6. Example of Colleen Barrett: every time I go against my gut, I regret it.
  7. Understand your personal biases and prejudices so your initial reaction doesn't eliminate excellent candidates.
  8. Example of Robin Bradford: she goes slower in an interview when she has a prejudice, and some of the best people she found were outside her comfort zone.
Strategy 12: Look for the Passionate Candidate
  1. Passion differentiates the exceptional candidate: she will love her job, she's dedicated, she will work hard.
  2. When passion is present, the sky is the limit. When it's missing, all bets are off.
  3. Key ingredients of passion are: energy and enthusiasm.
  4. Three more ideas to consider:
    1. Something to prove. Example of Warren Buffett who was rejected from Harvard Business School. Example of Paul Orfalea who is dyslexic - founded Kinko's.
    2. Athletes, immigrants, entrepreneurs (trust your instincts and past experience):
      1. Current passions bodes well for future passion on the job, especially amateur athletes (understand discipline, long hours, teamwork).
      2. It's illegal to ask about someone's origin, but immigrants can be dedicated to distinguish themselves in their new country.
      3. Strong entrepreneurial backgrounds can be a sign of passion.
    3. Hours. Does he have a track record of working long hours? Can be a sign of passion. Beware of: people that use it to compensate for lesser quality of work, that are self-destructive workaholics.
Strategy 13: Ask All Your Questions at Once
  1. You accomplish three things:
    1. You pass on the baton - performance depends on the candidate, not you selling yourself or the organization.
    2. Stops you from talking too much - and telegraphing the answers you want.
    3. Forces you to listen.
  2. Example of George Hume, CEO, handed over a sheet with 10 questions. With practice, he eventually became a comfortable, more animated listener.
Strategy 14: Have Fun During the Interview
  1. Ask 2 or 3 'Columbo' questions - the candidate should respond accordingly and you'll learn a lot.
  2. Examples:
    1. Are you lucky?
    2. What exactly does a ____ do? Listen for a clear, detailed explanation.
    3. How are you going to lose money for me?
    4. Example of Carl Sewell: looks for fidgeting - sign of energetic person.
  3. Listen for what is left out. Example of COO candidate in all-female board, he left out the women in his life during the interview and was a bad fit.
Strategy 15: Assign a Mini-Project to Finalists
  1. Three quarters through the interview give a task to perform. Demonstrates the candidates behavior and breaks the monotony of the interview.
  2. Examples:
    1. Advertising agency asked creative director candidate's opinion about feature with 92-year-old blues singer.
    2. Crate and Barrel marketing candidate asked about marketing new spring merchandise.
    3. United Electric Controls asks machinists to interpret a blueprint and mill or fabricate the part. Candidates appreciate the opportunity to demonstrate their skills.
  3. If an ad agency's candidate has a reel of past work condensed to 15 minutes, absolutely worth it to see. Tells more about his creative abilities than mini-project.
  4. All mini-projects (initiated by you or volunteered by the candidate) count toward the 200 plays you need to observe to pick the right person.
Strategy 16: Seek Closure by Announcing the Five-Minute Warning
  1. With the end in sight, performance changes.
  2. People will say something really important at this point, something like 'by the way...', 'I almost forgot...'
  3. If the interview was good, you may expect a variety of surprises, more about personal issues.
  4. Example of plant manager thought he would have to work weekends, which he didn't.
  5. Example of sales manager with sick child who needed health insurance.
Strategy 17: Watch for Inappropriate Behavior
  1. Pay attention to the candidate's behavior, not words, especially if you cannot explain it.
  2. Consider drugs and alcohol whenever you see inappropriate behavior in a qualified candidate.
  3. Example of university department chair who was offered the position of campus dean. He didn't stop talking about himself without pausing to inhale - which lit a red light. Eventually it was discovered he was an alcoholic.
Strategy 18: Identify Strengths & Weaknesses
  1. No one is good at everything, we all play to our strengths. But strengths in the extreme may predict weaknesses.
  2. Ask candidates about their strengths and consider what might exist on the opposite side.
  3. Example of well spoken, charismatic candidate: had possible problems with pressure, might avoid conflict, sweep problems under the rug.
  4. Example of airline test: one airline went by the book and couldn't handle spontaneous situations.
Strategy 19: Pick a Subject Where You Are the Expert
  1. Rule of thumb: if a candidates can go into detail about a subject, they probably have some expertise in that area. If not, the opposite is true.
  2. The key is to ask questions in your area of expertise in order to recognise another person's expertise.
  3. You can ask for a 5 minute summary on subjects you aren't expert on.
  4. Example of the question 'tell me about an employee you had to fire':
    1. One answered 'never fired anyone'.
    2. One said he found it very hard.
    3. One was direct - the employee wasn't performing so he fired him.
  5. Answer (1) from an experienced candidate should sound a warning bell. Answers (2) and (3) are good, depends on your style.
Strategy 20: Take Notes During the Interview
  1. Use a pad with a line down the middle - on one side write what the candidate tells you, on the other side write what you're thinking.
  2. Nothing is too minor to write down - thoughts, feelings, likes, dislikes.
  3. Write your notes according to what is legal to ask in an interview.
Strategy 21: Interview in Teams for Top Candidates
  1. Example of 5 executives trying to interview together as a new selection process.
  2. The candidate was very relaxed, only one of the team saw he was arrogant and didn't want him managing people in their company. Her view was accepted.
Strategy 22: Ask for a Legal Release
  1. Due to a surge in employment discrimination cases, have the candidate sign whatever releases are relevant.
  2. People hide things and make misrepresentations, which is grounds for termination.
Strategy 23: Throw a Few Curveballs at the End of the Interview
  1. Do something unpredictable after the interview, like walking the candidate to his car. Look for anything that says something personal about him.
  2. Example of odd candidate with messy car. Can he be a good manager?
    • Even if the car doesn't reveal anything, a person's behavior outside the office with the comfort of his car nearby may tell you something.
  3. Example of mid-thirties male candidate. His history was straightforward - close family, prep school, athlete and leader. Perfect presentation.
    • Only outside a question managed to extract his anger and emotions.
    • His wife was waiting 2 hours in the hot car.
    • He was hired against the recommendation, and his relationship with female employees was a disaster. Lasted less than a year.
Chapter 3: Strategies After the Interview
  1. Like in fishing, when hiring excellent people, half the battle remains after the candidate leaves your office. Like hooking the fish but not catching it.
  2. If you keep your eyes and ears open during the post-interview phase, you'll learn more about the candidate's behavior and track record than you ever thought possible.
Strategy 24: Ask for a Return Call from the Candidate
  1. Before a candidate leaves the office, ask him to call back on an agreed time and day. Quick and easy way to observe behavior.
  2. 15% of finalists miss the call.
  3. Example of contractor that didn't call back, which confirmed suspicions he wasn't disciplined enough to complete work on time.
Strategy 25: Assign a Take-Home Project
  1. Give finalists a current project and ask for an analysis.
  2. Evaluates attention to detail, ability to analyse problems and suggest solutions.
  3. Example of CFO finalist to advise on acquisition.
  4. Example of giving editor finalist an unedited chapter to edit and return.
  5. You will learn more about the candidate: how long did it take to return the material? Was it sent by priority mail? What shape was the material in?
  6. It gives them a chance to show you their best work.
Strategy 26: Travel with Finalists for Executive Positions
  1. One trip is worth a hundred interviews.
  2. Example of VP candidate who demonstrated he was a great problem solver, was always in good spirits. Then he became stubborn, which the founder thought was destructive.
    • He proved to be a tough negotiator to the asset of the company.
  3. Don't count someone out based on 1-2 plays. Be prepared for twists and turns.
Strategy 27: Meet the Spouse or Significant Other
  1. It's illegal to ask about a candidate's marital status, but it is widespread especially for executive positions.
  2. You may learn a lot, since the partner is a hidden player, without whose support a hire is doomed to failure.
  3. How does the candidate treat their spouse, and vice versa? Does the partner have worries and anxieties? These issues must be addressed fully before hiring, otherwise the employment will be short.
Strategy 28: Put Potential Problems on the Table
  1. Always have a final interview about potential problems - not if there will be problems, but what they will be.
  2. If you haven't discovered any, you're missing something in the candidate's background.
  3. Example of executive finalist whose husband was unemployed. She was very candid about their situation. She turned out to be very successful.
  4. Example of academic dean with migraines. The solution was a gym membership.
Strategy 29: Use an Intuitive Person in the Selection Process
  1. Can be colleague or friend. Examples of such experts: car salesman, shoe / clothing salesperson. Newspaper reporter.
  2. Example of secretary who rode up in the elevator with a candidate and knew right away he wasn't right for the job.
Strategy 30: Consider what Psychological Tests Have to Offer
  1. Psychological testing can help in the hiring process, especially if several converge on a potential problem.
  2. The skill of the evaluator is more important than the kind of test. The interpretation of the results can help or not - depends on the skill of the interpreter.
  3. Follow up with a conversation, see how the candidate reacts to the results - agree, disagree, laugh, elaborate?
  4. See if the results correspond with the candidate's history, interview impressions, behavior, etc.
Strategy 31: Experiment with Handwriting Analysis
  1. Graphology is only as good as the interpreter, and should be used only to supplement other methods.
  2. A short, inexpensive test: the candidate summarises in one page why he is good for the job, and signs it.
  3. Try this strategy, but don't rely on it - negative results should be considered as a flashing red light.
Chapter 4: Checking References
  1. No hire is better than a bad hire.
  2. References offer a photo album of strengths and weaknesses.
  3. We check references every day: we ask about good babysitters, mechanics, etc.
  4. When it comes to hiring, we balk, not wanting to hear negative information (especially if we like the candidate).
  5. When we do, we confirm facts instead of asking for information.
  6. Be persistent, eventually you will discover something. Ask references for references.
Strategy 32: Ask the References to Call You Back
  1. Simplest most effective way to check a reference - also legal with no derogatory information shared.
  2. Call at their lunchtime, so as to get an assistant or voice mail, leave the message "Call me back if the candidate was outstanding".
  3. If the candidate was outstanding, 8 out of ten people will respond quickly and want to help - consider this a green light.
  4. If only 2-3 out of ten references return your call, also a clear message.
Strategy 33: Network Up the Chain of Command
  1. References are often more valuable as you move up the chain of command.
  2. An employer's ability to hear bad news about a candidate is inversely proportional to the time invested in courting the employee. (Cognitive dissonance.)
  3. Two examples of calling high-up associates that gave bad reviews.
Strategy 34: Use the Internet as a Resource
  1. Research a person's record online for half an hour.
  2. Often, public information exists online that shows a candidate's past behaviour.
Strategy 35: Perform Due Diligence for All Finalists
  1. Credit checks and investigative reports on legal public information can predict future behaviour of a candidate.
    • Driving and financial records.
    • Real estate dealings.
    • Court appearances and litigation history.
  2. Examine carefully if the candidate is heavily in debt.
  3. Caveat: credit reports can be misleading. Candidates deserve the chance to tell their side of the story.
Strategy 36: Ask the Candidate, "What Will I Hear?"
  1. Always ask the candidate "What will I hear - positive and negative - when I call your references?"
  2. This alerts him you will be checking his references in depth, so he can give his side of the story.
  3. Candidates deserve the chance to explain the peaks and valleys of their work history.
  4. Example of graduate student in Japan who blew the whistle on corruption and saved the company.
Strategy 37: Devise a Phone Reference Checklist
  1. It's critical to listen without interruption, even with long pauses. The true feelings of references are hidden between their words, pauses and silences.
  2. Example checklist 1:
    1. Technical competence - can the candidate perform the required tasks?
    2. Intelligence - on a scale from 1 to 10.
    3. People skills - listen for silences, gaps.
    4. Motivation - your offer should be structured accordingly to his motivational needs (money, independence, meaningful assignments).
    5. Everything else - say "Is there anything I haven't asked?" and listen carefully.
  3. Example checklist 2:
    1. Did you like the person?
    2. What did he fail at doing?
    3. What was her reputation in the company?
    4. How did he communicate?
    5. What was his reputation in the industry?
    6. How did he react to authority?
    7. What was the reason for leaving?
    8. What was her level of energy, drive?
    9. What did she accomplish?
    10. What would you change about the person if you could?
Strategy 38: Meet References for the Finalists
  1. This is a rare strategy. The more personal your reference contact, the more likely you're to get honest information.
  2. Visit a key referral in their office to discuss a finalist.
  3. If the candidate isn't outstanding, the reference will decline an interview and not look you in the eye.
Chapter 5: Final Strategies
  1. All the hiring strategies were designed to save time, but they initially take time.
Strategy 39: Invest in People, Not Ideas
  1. The secret in business isn't money or technology or ideas, it's people.
  2. Example of store manager that was moved to another branch, and he achieved the same 20% better results.
Strategy 40: Find Someone You Trust
  1. The higher up you go in an organisation, the more likely employees are to keep from you bad news. Including bad hires.
  2. The leader may be subconsciously conveying "don't bring me bad news".
  3. It helps to have a trusted colleague to tell you when you're making a mistake.
  4. Example of art school president that hired the wrong VP, and everyone was intimidated into not saying anything.
Strategy 41: Follow These Three Cardinal Rules
  1. Follow a checklist of three question about the candidate:
    1. Is the candidate honest? If not, stop here.
    2. Has a clean record on crime and alcohol? Consult your lawyer and stick to public records.
    3. Is in good physical health?
Strategy 42: Ask Yourself These Ten Questions
  1. Your answer to each will reveal what you value in an employee:
    1. A repeated plaintiff where the candidate lived and worked? Investigate further. Twenty unpaid speeding tickets? Doesn't honor the rules.
    2. How does the candidate dress informally? Shorts and t-shirt, or three-piece suit?
    3. Are drug-free employees important?
    4. Does responsiveness count? Send him homework to study and call him two days later. Has he read the material and have questions?
    5. Should you create a model for hires? Give psychological tests to your employees to create a database of attributes for success.
    6. Do people like working for the candidate? Contact his current employees and ask.
    7. Can your current employees help find quality hires? Reward them for recommending someone that is hired.
    8. Is documentation important? Put all job offers in writing.
    9. Do you "hire" from other firms, suppliers and vendors? Adapt these strategies to select the best people from outside your organisation.
    10. Should you discard old hiring habits? Yes, add new tools and subtract outdated hiring practices.
Strategy 43: Use Yourself as a Test Case with Experts
  1. Search online your own public records: driving, mortgage, credit.
  2. Use yourself as a guinea pig in a psych or handwriting test to see if they are accurate.
  3. If useful, use them. If not, don't use them.
Strategy 44: Suggest a Trial Run When Possible
  1. When undecided about a person:
    1. Give the candidate a longer assignment.
    2. Hire on a temporary basis.
  2. Watch the person under game conditions. Red and green lights will become clearer.
Strategy 45: Design Your Own Hiring System
  1. Choose the tools that you like best.
  2. Design a flowchart on a single page that summarises the best hiring strategies for you (page 165).
