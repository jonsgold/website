Book Summary
Introduction

Chris Rock begins developing material for a new show by doing around fifty appearances in front of several tens of people, using a yellow legal pad with ideas scribbled on it.

His style during these appearances is sometimes painful to watch, not at all like his performance mode. He can ramble, most jokes falling flat. However, he watches the audience intently, looking for clues as to which ideas might have potential, adding notes to his pad. What he thought was a good idea might completely miss with the audience. Other times, a joke he thought was a dud will bring the house down.

Rock understands ingenious ideas don't spring into the mind fully formed. They emerge through a rigorous experimental process.

As an investor, the author, Peter Sims, learned the most sucessful entrepreneurs don't begin with brilliant ideas, they discover them.

Examples:

  • The original goal of Google's founders was to solve a small problem: how to prioritize library searches online. This led to their famous PageRank algorithm.
  • Google discovered that their AdWords advertising system was profitable, but they didn't begin with the idea.
  • Amazon has a culture of discovery, actually trying new things and ideas is part of employee performance reviews. Jeff Bezos knows you must experiment, you can't predict which ideas will succeed.

Some ideas don't work. Amazon's history has many features the customers didn't care about. Other ideas exceed expectations. Amazon has endured criticism over the years for its failed experiments, however ultimately, the exploratory mentality has spawned continual breakthroughs, such as Amazon stores and Amazon Web Services (AWS).


There are two types of innovators: conceptual and experimental. Conceptual innovators (like Mozart) pursue bold new ideas, often achieving breakthroughs early in life. Such prodigies are rare.

Experimental innovators (like Rock, Google and Bezos) approach problems using little bets. This type of creativity is more common. They use iterative, trial and error approaches to gradually build up breakthroughs. They must be persistent and willing to accept failure.

The advantage of this method is we rarely know what we don't know. Successful inventors and creators understand this. Beethoven used experiments to slowly differentiate his style from Mozart's, creating a distinct style. They don't analyze new ideas too much or try to predict their success with elaborate plans. They do things to discover what do to.

Little bets are concrete actions taken to discover, test and develop ideas that are achievable and affordable. When we can't know what's going to happen, little bets help us learn about the factors that can't be understood beforehand. Most successful entrepreneurs, especially those who start with limited capital, operate in this experimental way.

Little bets are not ways of just trying a lot of things to see what sticks. The most productive creative teams are rigorous, analytical, strategic and pragmatic. Even so, this method is not a step by step process.

Fundamentals of the little bets approach:

  • Experiment: learn by doing, fail quickly to learn fast.
  • Play: quiet inhibitions to incubating ideas with a playful, improvisational and humorous atmosphere.
  • Immerse: get out into the world to gather fresh ideas, understand human desires, absorb how things work ground up.
  • Define: use insights to define specific problems before solving them.
  • Reorient: be flexible in pursuit of larger goals, use small wins to pivot and rechart the course when needed.
  • Iterate: repeat, refine and test frequently using the insights gained from prior iterations.

Chapter 1: Big Bets vs Little Bets

The tyranny of large numbers: the tendency to think in terms of bigger bets as you get bigger. In the 1990s, Hewlett-Packard were looking only at billion dollar markets in order to keep growing rapidly. All their big ideas failed because someone was already in that market. They weren't discovering new opportunities or developing new products, they were relying on the successes of competitors to identify targets.

HP had been a creative and experimental company before. In 1972, they created the HP-35 when the market for scientific calculators did not yet exist. Market research said it wouldn't sell, but Bill Hewlett decided to build a thousand and see what happens. It was a small, affordable bet, and within a few months they were selling a thousand calculators a day.

The US army tactics against the Soviets during the cold war were doctrinal, memorizing checklists. This centralized decision making didn't work well in Vietnam - increased bombing didn't reduce Viet Cong resistance. The assumption seemed logical as a plan, but hadn't been validated in the real world.

The US army had the same problem fighting adaptive guerilla warfare in the Middle East. They had to change their approach to one of discovery and experimentation by immersing with the local population to understand the local power structure and identify people they can rely on. With the gained intelligence, the army's plans could evolve. To make this approach work, they had to acknowledge mistakes would be made, and make it cool.

Two advantages of the little bets approach:

  • It enables us to focus on what we can afford to lose instead of making assumptions on how much we can gain.
  • It facilitates the development of means as we progress with an idea, defined as:
    • Who we are: our values and tastes.
    • What we know: our expertise, knowledge and skills.
    • Who we know: our networks, friends and allies.
    • Monetary resources.

Pixar illustrates this approach. In the mid 1980s, Pixar was a hardware company that took little bets on making short films. They were affordable losses which also allowed the company to develop its means, including their digital animation expertise, reputation and technology, as well as the ability to tell a good story. Eventually they released Toy Story in 1994 to great success.

The little bets approach involves failure, sometimes a lot of it. Chris Rock is willing to bomb in front of a small audience night after night. Only six out of every 100 new HP products were successes.
