Introduction

Core Idea: Big successes often start with small, experimental ideas - "little bets" - that evolve through iteration, discovery, and adaptation.

Key Examples: Stories from Steve Jobs, Chris Rock, and Pixar illustrate how innovators use small experiments to refine ideas.

Chris Rock develops material for a new show in small clubs. Most jokes fall flat, but he watches the audience intently, looking for clues as to which ideas have potential. What he thought was a good idea might completely miss. Other times, a joke he thought was a dud will bring the house down. He understands ingenious ideas don't spring into the mind fully formed. They emerge through a rigorous experimental process.

As an investor, the author, Peter Sims, learned the most sucessful entrepreneurs don't begin with brilliant ideas, they discover them.

Examples:

  • The original goal of Google's founders was to solve a small problem: how to prioritize library searches online. This led to their famous PageRank algorithm.
  • Google discovered that their AdWords advertising system was profitable, but they didn't begin with the idea.
  • Amazon has a culture of discovery. You need to experiment with ideas, you can't predict which will succeed.

Some ideas don't work - Amazon's history has many features the customers didn't care about. Some ideas exceed expectations - Amazon's exploratory mentality has spawned continual breakthroughs, such as Amazon stores and Amazon Web Services (AWS).


There are two types of innovators: conceptual and experimental. Conceptual innovators are rare, they pursue bold new ideas, often achieving breakthroughs early in life. An example is Mozart.

Experimental innovators (like Rock, Google and Bezos) approach problems using little bets. This type of creativity is more common. They use iterative, trial and error approaches to gradually build up breakthroughs. They are persistent and willing to accept failure.

The advantage of this method is we rarely know what we don't know. Successful creators don't analyze new ideas and plan too much - they do things in order to discover what do to. They test and develop ideas by taking concrete actions that are achievable and affordable.

Fundamentals of the little bets approach:

  • Experiment: learn by doing, fail quickly to learn fast.
  • Play: quiet inhibitions to incubating ideas with a playful, improvisational and humorous atmosphere.
  • Immerse: get out into the world to gather fresh ideas, understand human desires, absorb how things work ground up.
  • Define: use insights to define specific problems before solving them.
  • Reorient: be flexible in pursuit of larger goals, use small wins to pivot and rechart the course when needed.
  • Iterate: repeat, refine and test frequently using the insights gained from prior iterations.

Chapter 1: Big Bets vs Little Bets

Main Point: Relying solely on big, high-risk plans can stifle creativity. Instead, using small, low-risk bets encourages experimentation and learning.

Example: Chris Rock tries out jokes in small clubs before incorporating them into big shows.

The tyranny of large numbers: the tendency to think in terms of bigger bets as you get bigger. 1990s Hewlett-Packard were trying new ideas only in billion dollar markets. They failed because someone was already in that market.

HP had once been a creative company. In 1972, they made the first scientific calculator HP-35. Market research said it wouldn't sell, but Bill Hewlett decided to build a thousand and see what would happen. It was a small, affordable bet, and within a few months they were selling a thousand calculators a day.

Fighting the adaptive guerilla warfare of the Middle East, the US army had to change their approach. They immersed themselves with the local population to understand the power structure and identify people they can rely on. They made small incremental discoveries, gained intelligence so the army's plans could evolve.

Two advantages of the little bets approach:

  • It enables us to focus on what we can afford to lose instead of making assumptions on how much we can gain.
  • It facilitates the development of means as we progress with an idea:
    • Who we are: our values and tastes.
    • What we know: our expertise, knowledge and skills.
    • Who we know: our networks, friends and allies.
    • Monetary resources.

1980s Pixar was a hardware company that took little bets on making short films. They were affordable losses which also allowed the company to develop its means, including their digital animation expertise, reputation and technology, as well as the ability to tell a good story. Eventually they released Toy Story in 1994 to great success.

The little bets approach involves failure, sometimes a lot of it. Chris Rock is willing to bomb in front of a small audience night after night. Only six out of every 100 new HP products were successes.

Chapter 2: The Growth Mind-set

Main Point: Adopting a "growth mindset" (from Carol Dweck's research) helps people embrace challenges, learn from failures, and continuously improve.

Application: Little bets thrive in an environment where mistakes are seen as opportunities to grow.

A big vision provides direction and inspiration, but experimental innovativors aren't rigid in pursuit of that vision. They accept that they may need to redefine the path or even the ultimate goal. They know that by making mistakes they will make important discoveries.

Carol Dweck's research shows that people with a growth mind-set are more likely to succeed than those with a fixed mind-set:

  • Growth mind-set people believe intelligence and abilities can be grown through effort. They view setbacks as opportunities for growth. They tend to seek activities that expand their abilities. They don't believe their performance on every task reflects on their intelligence.
  • Fixed mind-set people believe intelligence and abilities are set in stone, thus they need to prove those abilities. They view failures as threatening to their sense of worth. They tend to gravitate to activities that confirm their abilities.

Pixar is an example of a growth mind-set. After several successful movies, they shook things up to make sure they weren't becoming complacent. Pixar fosters this mind-set by viewing failures as their modus operandi. The key is to systematically learn from failures, and closely monitor what's working and what isn't.

The little bets approach helps us to cultivate the growth mind-set. It's learnable - by doing, rather than planning, we can develop growth mind-set muscles.

Chapter 3: Failing Quickly to Learn Fast

Main Point: Failure isn't the enemy - it's a necessary part of innovation. The faster you fail and learn, the quicker you improve.

Examples: Designers and entrepreneurs create prototypes quickly to test and refine ideas early.

Being rigorous about spotting flaws is essential to creative achievement, but don't let perfectionism be paralyzing. There are two forms of perfectionism: healthy and unhealthy.

  • Healthy perfectionism is about setting high standards for yourself and others, planning ahead and strong organizational skills. It's internally driven by a desire for quality and excellence. It indicates a high level of psychological well-being and satisfaction.
  • Unhealthy perfectionism is externally driven by things like parental pressures and needing approval. It causes a tendency to ruminate over past performances, or worry for making mistakes. It leads to depression, anxiety and eating disorders.

Prototyping: be wrong as fast as you can. Fail quickly in order to learn fast by creating low-cost, rough prototypes. Entrepreneurs push ideas into the market as quickly as possible in order to learn from mistakes.

Prototyping also overcomes the blank page problem. Write a shitty first draft, no matter how bad, and improve on the next drafts. It is effective to jump start our thinking and to guide us to an experimental approach.

Pixar uses storyboards to prototype their movies in a series of sketches. This first version sucks, but it's easier and cheaper to spot problems and correct them early. Less time and emotion was invested, and potential users are more comfortable sharing their honest reactions when they see a rough prototype.

Chapter 4: The Genius of Play

Main Point: Playfulness fosters creativity by encouraging exploration without fear of failure.

Examples: Toy designers and creative thinkers use playful methods to unlock ideas.

Creating an atmosphere that allows for playfulness and improvisation is one of the most effective ways to inspire the experimentation that leads to the best ideas and insights. A study on musicians playing improvised jazz in MRI machines showed that the part of the brain responsible for evaluating and censoring switched off. Improvising unlocks a creative state of mind.

This is called being in a state of flow. We are more likely to experience flow when we do work that appeals to our intrinsic interests that's also aligned with our personal strengths. It's rare to be in this state, as negative perfectionism, fear, self-doubt and self-censoring are obstacles to flow.

Improvisational techniques can counteract these barriers:

  • Accept every offer. Nothing is too silly. Use "yes... and" language which builds up ideas. Don't use "yes... but" language which implies judgment.
  • Make your partner look good. It creates positive energy that drives improvisation, and reduces inhibitions and doubts. It lets you relax and be playful, get in a zone.

Pixar calls these techniques plussing, used to improve ideas without using judgmental language. They give feedback constructively, in an upbeat manner. It requires that people let go of the need to control every detail and rely on the talents of others, but also allows for giving pointed critique where needed.

Another aspect of playfulness is humor. Studies indicate that humor creates positive group effects and opens up our inventive juices. It increases trust, thus increasing the quantity and quality of group communications.

Successful humor (not demeaning or derogatory) breaks down the power structures that tend to inhibit tighter social bonds. The HiPPO effect (the highest paid person's opinion) is the fallacy that the best answers come from the most senior person, but often there's little correlation.

Practice: Use "problem-finding" techniques to uncover real needs and opportunities.

Strategy: Seek input from varied fields, users, and experiences to fuel innovation.
